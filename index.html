Capstone Project with Squirrel AI

Overview

This capstone project is part of the METALS program at Carnegie Mellon University. Our team partnered with Squirrel AI to improve the learning and user experience of their online learning platform. Through a rigorous research process, including qualitative research with experts, teachers, and learners and quantitative research with educational data mining, we’ve identified the needs and challenges of the users and thus some possible areas of improvement for the mistake analysis system on the Squirrel AI online learning platform. Our proposed solution will help middle school students to analyze, understand, and reflect on their mistakes; thereby developing their meta-cognitive skills.

Background

The specific area that we focused on is the mistake analysis feature. With this feature, students can do Mistake Analysis Related Studies (MARS) like correcting and analyzing mistakes and reviewing related knowledge components (KC’s). This is an attempt to translate the traditional MARS experience from physical notebooks to online.

Currently, the MARS system is mainly used as an assignment to the students:

  1. Teachers will assign the mistaken problems that students got in a particular class session.
  2. Students will practice solving those problems and bring back their revised solutions.
  3. Teachers will check the revised solutions and provide further feedback.

Before the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic, students printed out the problem sets and completed them in school because they did not have access to the internet at school. During the COVID-19 pandemic, students can complete the assignment online at home.

Research Phase

Research Process Overview

Preliminary Research

Narrow Down Our Scope

We identified 3 major potential directions after the examination of the user flow and selected Mistake-Analysis Related Study as our final direction.
We first examined the user flow of the existing system, then came up with three major potential directions. After evaluating the feasibility and the scope, we decided to take the mistake analysis related study as our focus.

Stakeholder Map

This model helps us to better understand how different groups influence the learning experience of the students and where we can get the resources for our design & research.

User Research

Understanding Experts

We interviewed 4 students with rich experience in making physical mistake analysis notebooks and 6 learning experts regarding mistake analysis notebooks. These interviews helped us understand the process, value, and challenges of MARS.

With the interview data with expert students, we created an experience map showing the process of expert students utilizing MARS to support their learning. With the experience map, we identified the following problems:

  • Students lack internal & external motivation to start and persist
  • Students need metacognitive skills for selection & review & disposition (see the value)
  • Time can be a problem for students to do & review mistake analysis notebook

We used an affinity diagram to code and group similar ideas from the interviews in a bottom-up process. From this affinity diagram, we’ve gained the following insights:

  • Students don't have the ability to reason and categorize mistakes
  • Current online-offline integration still needs improvements as online is deemed not effective enough
  • Students lack motivation and are not interested and mistake analysis notebook becomes a formality

Understanding Students

We conducted interviews with 6 students, grade levels vary from 7th grade to 10th grade, Generally rank in the middle of the class, to understand their learning process and the pain points they have during their online learning.

Based on the raw data we acquired from our users, we developed an identity model to reveal the identity and behaviors of our learners. The model is divided into 4 sections: I am, I like, I do, and I struggle.

From the identity model, we generalized these insights that suggest the potential challenges and opportunities that can be considered in ideation.

  • Students need clear explanations to tackle misconceptions and learn better problem-solving strategies.
  • Internet access and synchronization with knowledge at school are the barriers to combine online and offline study.
  • System cannot identify the problems that a student has already mastered.
  • Certain features are not used fully.
  • Students have limited ability to do mistake reasoning on their own.

Understanding Teachers

We also interviewed 5 math/physics teachers and 2 monitors at Squirrel AI to understand their needs, challenges, and thoughts on the current system.

With the interview data, we created an experience map showing the process of teachers helping students to learn from MARS. With the experience map, we identified the following problems:

  • Repetitive problems due to limited problem types may leave students to learn by rote.
  • It’s difficult to manage and organize problems in the system.
  • Students have a heavy workload because of the number of practice questions.
  • Teachers and students both consider MARS as homework instead of a tool for review and metacognition.
  • Teachers struggle to monitor students’ attitudes and understanding of problem-solving.

Exploratory Data Analysis

We analyzed mistake labeling log data from 3 million events. Through the system-auto labeled data, we found out that the system default label system causes student’s cognitive overload, as there are:

  • altogether 35 given labels to select
  • at least 18 labels shown in the drop-down window
Students’ self-labeling results show that students are lacking in MARS required meta-cognitive knowledge, as the most frequent labelings are “do not know” & “not my fault”. This implicated that students are not capable of analyzing their mistakes. Therefore, Students need scaffolding to help them develop MARS metacognitive level knowledge.

Ideation

From group brainstorming, building up difficulty & importance matrix, and speed dating with users (students), teachers, and clients, we finalized ideation paths towards prototyping.

Based on the 5 insights generated from the research phase, we raised over 50 problems starting with “how might we, we come out with 34 initial ideas from these 50 “how might we” statements. With 41 initial ideas generated, we evaluated these ideas by their implementation difficulties and importance for our users. According to the Difficulty & Importance Matrix, we selected 14 ideas in the first quadrant for speed dating. After 2 rounds of speed dating with 11 users of Squirrel AI system, we identified 5 concept directions for the conclusion of spring semester:

  1. Q&A channel for students and teachers to communicate with each other offline.
  2. An online assistant that helps students with problem-solving skills and finding relevant knowledge components without teachers’ help.
  3. Starred question set that gathers questions that students identify as important, system recommends and teachers recognize as important.
  4. Key knowledge component identification that helps students with more practice of important knowledge components and thus improve their metacognitive thinking.
  5. Pre-formatted problem sets for easy scanning that supports online study complement to offline study.
